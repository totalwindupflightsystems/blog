---

I walk through Bogotá and see it everywhere. On the TransMilenio, a woman in business attire has a ChatGPT window open, scrolling through a thread — not asking one question and closing it, but *reading*. A conversation. On a café table in Chapinero, a student has Copilot pulled up next to a half-written essay, the two windows side by side like they belong together. In line at Éxito, a man is talking to his phone — not on a call, the screen is too bright for that. He's dictating something to an AI. "How do I cook the chicken so it doesn't dry out," he says, and the phone speaks back.

This isn't tech workers. This isn't early adopters. This is everyone.

My partner talks about what the AI told them. Not in the way people talk about a useful tool — "oh, I used the calculator" — but in the way people talk about a conversation. "ChatGPT said I should try restructuring the argument first, and then it gave me three examples." The verb "said." Not "generated." Not "returned." Said. As though there's a person on the other end delivering counsel.

A friend of mine, who has never written a line of code and has no interest in doing so, now spends twenty minutes a day talking to Claude. Not for work. Not for productivity. She asks it questions about her garden. She tells it about her day. She describes it as "useful" in the same tone she uses for her therapist. The AI is not a search engine with better formatting. It's a presence. One she consults regularly and references in conversation the way people used to reference what they saw on Twitter or what someone posted on Instagram.

Something has shifted.

## The Social Evidence

For two decades, the standard image of someone on their phone in public was a person scrolling — Instagram, Twitter, TikTok, whatever feed dominated that year. The face was passive. The thumb moved up. The content washed over them.

What I see now is different. People aren't scrolling. They're typing. They're reading. They're looking at a screen and then looking up, thinking about what they just read, then looking back down and responding. It's not consumption. It's interaction. The physical posture is different — it's the posture of someone in a text conversation with a person they care about, not someone doomscrolling a feed.

I've started noticing it everywhere. The woman on the train who's clearly asking ChatGPT for advice before a meeting. The student in the library who has Perplexity open and is cross-referencing something across tabs. The couple at a restaurant where one person pulls out their phone, types something into an AI, and then shows the other person the response — and they both react, discuss it, argue with it.

The AI output has become something you *show* someone. The way you used to show someone a text from your mom, or a funny tweet, or a photo. "Look what it said." The AI's response is social currency.

## Programming Was Never This Hot — Not as a Job, But as a Thing People Do

There's a strange reversal happening. For years, "learn to code" was career advice — a path to a stable job, a good salary, an in-demand skill. Coding was hot because coding was lucrative.

Now coding is hot because it's *interesting*. Not as a career — as an activity. As a thing people do for the same reason they watch cooking shows or follow sports. You might never write a line of production code. But you've used an AI to build something — a personal dashboard, a script that renames your photos, a website for your friend's wedding. You've watched YouTube reviews of AI coding tools the way you watch car reviews. You have opinions about which model "codes best" despite never deploying anything to production. You're a spectator, but the line between spectator and participant is thin enough that everyone has tried it at least once.

The AI coding influencers and the car review channels are the visible part of this, but they're riding a wave that's much bigger than they are. The wave is: **interacting with AI has become a normal human activity.** It's not a developer thing. It's not a tech thing. It's what people do when they're waiting for the bus.

When something becomes a normal human activity — the way texting became normal, the way googling became normal — it stops being a thing you do for a reason and becomes a thing you do because you have a minute. The friction is gone. The barrier is gone. The need for a specific use case is gone. You just... talk to it. About anything. About everything. Because it's there, and it answers, and people have started to care about what it says.

## What People Actually Talk About

The conversations I overhear are not technical. Nobody on the TransMilenio is discussing the SWE-Bench Pro implications of GPT-5.6's tokenizer architecture. They're asking whether the chicken needs more time in the oven. They're preparing for a difficult conversation with their boss. They're trying to understand a medical term their doctor used. They're arguing with the AI about a sports statistic, and the AI is winning, and the person is laughing.

The content of these interactions reveals something the benchmark race obscures: most people using AI are not evaluating its reasoning capability. They're evaluating its *presence*. Is it helpful? Does it understand what I'm asking? Does it talk to me like a person or like a manual? The technical benchmarks matter to the people building the models. The vibe matters to everyone else.

And the vibe, in mid-2026, is good enough that millions of people have made talking to an AI part of their daily rhythm. Not as a power user. Not as an early adopter. As a normal person doing normal things — cooking, commuting, preparing for meetings, settling arguments, looking things up — with an AI window open while they do it.

The strangest thing about this normalization is how fast it happened. In 2022, using an AI assistant was weird. In 2024, it was a developer thing. In 2026, my partner talks about what ChatGPT said the way they talk about what their friend said. The verb "said" implies a speaker. And people have started to hear one.

---

*This is an observation piece, not a research paper. Its evidence is walking through cities, sitting in cafés, listening to the people around me, and noticing that the way people use technology has changed faster than the language we have to describe it. The AI is no longer a tool people use. It's a presence people consult, reference, and talk about — as though it were another person in the room.*
