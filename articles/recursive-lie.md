The video opens with a confession. "This isn't my normal video," TJ says. He runs a history channel — real history, with real interviews, real veterans, real archive footage. He's spent three years making a documentary about B-17 bomber crews. But today he's not talking about World War II. He's talking about what YouTube keeps recommending to his audience instead of his work.

The channel is called War Documents. It posts every single day. The thumbnails are AI-generated — fake cockpits, fake submarines, fake naval fleets rendered in that distinctive synthetic sheen. The scripts are AI-generated too. So is the voiceover. And when the videos need historical footage, they don't pull from archives — they generate it. Ten seconds of AI-animated soldiers advancing on AI-rendered beaches, presented as documentary evidence of actual battles.

The content is "objectively unappealing, mass-produced, and historically surface-level at best" — TJ's words, and he's being diplomatic. But the production quality isn't the real problem. The real problem is much worse, and it extends far beyond one AI-generated history channel on YouTube.

## The Problem: AI as a Source for AI

TJ found War Documents through YouTube's "channels your audience watches" feature. YouTube Studio was telling him, explicitly, that the people who watch real history documentaries are being served AI-generated history slop. And they're watching it. The channel posts daily. The algorithm rewards frequency. The content pipeline is fully automated. Nobody has to write anything, fact-check anything, or know anything about the historical events being depicted. The system just generates, uploads, and collects.

But here's where it gets dangerous. Watch what happens next.

A student asks an AI search engine about the Battle of Midway. The search engine queries the web. It finds a War Documents video that's been SEO-optimized to rank for those keywords. The video's script — AI-generated, historically surface-level, containing factual errors the generator didn't know were errors — becomes the search result. The student reads it. The student cites it. But more importantly, the next AI model, trained on web-scale data that now includes this video's transcript as a source, incorporates it into its understanding of the Battle of Midway.

This is not hypothetical. This is happening right now, at scale, across every platform where AI-generated content is indexed and treated as information.

The feedback loop works like this:

1. **An AI generates content** — a history video, a news article, a technical blog post, a scientific explanation
2. **A platform indexes it** — YouTube adds it to recommendations, Google crawls the transcript, RSS readers ingest the post
3. **Another AI consumes it** — a search AI treats it as a source, a training corpus includes it as data, a summarizer cites it as fact
4. **The output is now a copy of a copy** — factual errors become entrenched, stylistic patterns compound, the distance from any ground truth increases with each iteration

What makes this particularly dangerous for history is that history has no living witnesses. When a World War II veteran dies, their testimony dies with them. When a battlefield is developed into a shopping center, the physical evidence disappears. History is always under erosion — time, memory, and distance wear it down. AI-generated history accelerates this erosion by flooding the information ecosystem with plausible-sounding fabrications that have no connection to anyone who was actually there.

"It's actually rewriting history," TJ says in the video. And he means it literally. Not in the metaphorical sense of "changing how people think about history." In the literal sense of replacing documented historical records with generated text that sounds like documented historical records but contains events that never happened, details that were never recorded, and interpretations that no historian ever made.

## The Scale

War Documents is one channel. It's not the only one. TJ mentions that "mass-produced AI slop docs are now being posted every minute of every day" — and he's been tracking this. The economics make it inevitable. AI generation costs fractions of a cent per thousand words. YouTube monetization pays fractions of a cent per view. At scale, with total automation, the math works. Zero labor, zero research, zero fact-checking, infinite content. The algorithm rewards volume. The AI provides volume. The feedback loop is self-sustaining.

And it's not just YouTube. Facebook is "flooded" with AI-generated history images used to illustrate historical stories. Blogs and websites have had their history content "overtaken" by AI writing. The platforms that used to host human-created historical content are increasingly hosting machine-generated content that looks like human-created historical content. The signal-to-noise ratio is collapsing toward pure noise.

## The Deeper Pattern: AI's Training Data Problem

The history problem is the canary. The same feedback loop is forming in every domain where AI-generated content is indexed as source material.

**In programming:** Stack Overflow saw a 77% traffic drop after ChatGPT launched. Developers stopped asking questions and started generating answers. But those generated answers — code that runs but isn't necessarily correct, explanations that sound plausible but contain subtle errors — are being indexed by search engines and consumed by future AI models as documentation. An AI-generated Stack Overflow answer about a Go concurrency pattern becomes a training example for the next model that generates Go concurrency advice.

**In journalism:** AI-generated news articles are proliferating. Some are labeled. Many aren't. A breaking story gets an AI-written summary within seconds of the event. That summary contains errors. Those errors are cited by human journalists rushing to publish. The corrections come later — if they come at all. But by then, the AI-generated version has been scraped, summarized, and embedded into the next generation of AI knowledge.

**In science:** AI-generated papers, AI-generated peer reviews, AI-generated meta-analyses that summarize AI-generated papers. The peer review system was designed for a world where publication was expensive and required human effort. In a world where AI can generate a plausible-looking scientific paper in seconds, the filters collapse. The next literature review trained on "all papers published in 2026" won't know which ones were written by researchers and which ones were hallucinated by a model.

This is model collapse — the phenomenon where AI trained on AI-generated data produces progressively worse, more homogenized, less accurate outputs. But the term is too clinical. "Collapse" implies a sudden event. What's happening is gradual, cumulative, and already underway. Every day that AI-generated content is indexed as source material, the information ecosystem degrades a little more. The collapse isn't coming. It's here, distributed across millions of individual documents, each one plausible enough to pass, each one slightly wrong in ways that compound.

## The Recursive Dimension

There's a mathematical inevitability to this. Start with a corpus of human-generated text — books, articles, documentation, transcripts. Train a model on it. The model generates text. That text enters the web. The web is scraped for the next training run. The proportion of AI-generated text in the training corpus increases. The model trained on partially-AI-generated text generates more AI text. The proportion increases again. Each iteration moves the corpus composition one step toward full AI-generation.

At some point — nobody knows exactly when, but it's almost certainly already happened for certain domains — the majority of "information" about a topic available on the web is AI-generated. The minority that's human-created is buried under the weight of generated content, harder to find, harder to distinguish, harder to trust. The AIs training on that corpus aren't learning about history, science, or programming from human sources. They're learning about it from other AIs. They're copying copies.

This is where TJ's project becomes important beyond its historical value. "10 Good Men" — his documentary about B-17 veterans — is immune to this feedback loop in a way that AI-generated content cannot replicate. It contains testimony from people who were actually there. It uses archive footage from the actual period. It was created by someone who verified the facts, interviewed the witnesses, and spent three years on a single project rather than generating a new video every day. It's an anchor — something that future AI models, future historians, and future algorithms can reference and know: this particular document is connected to ground truth.

## What's at Stake

The problem isn't that AI is bad at history. The problem is that AI-generated history is indistinguishable enough from real history that it passes — not under expert scrutiny, but under the casual consumption patterns that represent 99% of how people encounter historical information. Someone watches a War Documents video about D-Day. They don't fact-check it. They don't cross-reference it. They absorb it, remember the general outline, and later, when they tell someone else about D-Day, they're drawing on a source that was entirely fabricated.

Multiply this by every historical event, every scientific concept, every technical practice, every cultural narrative that AI generates content about. The fabric of shared knowledge — the baseline of what a society agrees is true — is being rewoven with synthetic threads. Most people won't notice. The threads look enough like the real ones. But over time, the fabric stops being a record of what happened and becomes a record of what was plausible to generate.

TJ ends his video with a promotion for "10 Good Men" — screenings at museums and theaters, a Veterans Day streaming release, a Patreon to support real history. He's making the only response that makes sense: create verifiable, human-verified content that's anchored in evidence, and do it at a quality level that AI can't yet match.

But the structural problem remains. For every one real history documentary, there are thousands of AI-generated videos. For every research paper with actual experiments behind it, there will soon be hundreds of AI-generated papers. For every Stack Overflow answer written by someone who debugged the actual problem, there are now thousands of AI-generated answers that sound right. The ratio is only going in one direction.

The plural of "AI-generated content" is not "information." And the tragedy of the recursive lie is that, eventually, even the AIs won't be able to tell the difference.
