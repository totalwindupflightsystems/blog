# The Container Chooses the Story

I saw a YouTube comment yesterday that cut to the bone. Someone was defending the 8-episode streaming season — the format that has become the default for everything from Star Wars to Marvel to Netflix originals. The argument was: if a story needs 8 episodes, use 8. If it needs 24, use 24. The problem only arises when the wrong format is forced on the right story.

And someone replied: "The problem we have is that a feature length movie is being bloated to an 8 episode series. And also the reverse — an 8 episode series being compressed to a feature film."

That's it. That's the entire structural problem with modern franchise storytelling in two sentences. The format doesn't follow the story. The format is chosen first — by executives, by budgets, by platform strategy — and then the story is retrofitted to fill it. Writers spend their careers pretending the container was their idea.

## Obi-Wan: A Trilogy Crammed Into Six Episodes

Stuart Beattie wrote *Obi-Wan Kenobi* as a movie trilogy. Not a show. Three films. The first script was complete. Beattie knew where the second and third would go — Obi-Wan's guilt over Anakin, his fractured relationship with the Force, his quiet protection of a young Luke Skywalker from a distance. He [told IGN](https://www.ign.com/articles/star-wars-obi-wan-kenobi-film-trilogy): "I was devastated when the trilogy was scrapped."

What scrapped it? *Solo: A Star Wars Story* underperformed at the box office. Lucasfilm [panicked](https://www.digitalspy.com/movies/a40454547/obi-wan-kenobi-movie-trilogy-scrapped/) and pivoted all Star Wars storytelling toward Disney+ series. The Boba Fett movie became *The Book of Boba Fett*. The Obi-Wan trilogy became a six-episode limited series. Same characters, same actors, different container.

Watch the show and you can feel the padding. A movie's worth of story stretched across six hours. The middle episodes meander through side quests and rescue missions that exist because the runtime demanded them, not because the story needed them. The writers were handed a container and told to fill it.

Beattie's [original vision](https://screenrant.com/star-wars-obi-wan-kenobi-trilogy-scrapped-plans/) was darker — Obi-Wan weighed down by guilt, his connection to the Force damaged, his relationship with Luke a quiet act of penance rather than mentorship. Some of that survived. Most of it dissolved into the structural requirements of a format the story was never designed for.

## Mando & Grogu: A Season Compressed Into a Film

The reverse happened to *The Mandalorian*. Three seasons of episodic television — a show built on the rhythm of a problem arriving, being solved, and the characters moving on. Season 4 was in development. Then [Disney announced](https://thedirect.com/article/the-mandalorian-movie-disney-show-changes) that Season 4 was scrapped and reworked into a feature film: *The Mandalorian & Grogu*, releasing May 2026.

The show's structure was [designed for streaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mandalorian_and_Grogu): episodic arcs, monster-of-the-week stakes, character development spread across hours of small moments. A theatrical film requires a different grammar — rising action, a central antagonist, a climax that pays off in under two hours. The writers now have to compress a season's worth of pacing into something that works in a single sitting.

The problem isn't that either format is inherently superior. The problem is that the format was chosen before anyone asked what format the story needed.

## The Hobbit: Two Films Become Three

Peter Jackson originally planned *The Hobbit* as two films. Guillermo del Toro was attached to direct. The production [became three films](https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/movies/movie-news/hobbit-peter-jackson-three-films-353122/) after Jackson took over and the studio saw the *Lord of the Rings* box office returns. One modest novel, shorter than any single volume of *Lord of the Rings*, stretched across nearly nine hours.

The result is a film trilogy that has to invent material to justify its runtime. An elf-dwarf love triangle. A pointless Laketown political subplot. Chases that go nowhere. The studio wanted three *Lord of the Rings*-scale events. The source material was a children's book.

## Andor: The Exception That Proves the Rule

Tony Gilroy wrote *Andor* for 12 episodes. Not 6, not 8 — 12. Structured as four three-episode arcs across two seasons. He [had the leverage](https://www.reddit.com/r/andor/comments/1qvoy5s/how_did_andor_seasons_get_12_full_length_episodes/) to demand the format he wanted. Kathleen Kennedy — whose greatest strength and weakness, as fans note, is giving total creative control to artists she trusts — let him have it.

The result is the most critically acclaimed Star Wars project since the original trilogy. The format serves the story. Each three-episode block has its own rising action, its own climax, its own resolution, while feeding into the larger arc. The [Los Angeles Times](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/tv/story/2025-04-22/andor-season-2-tony-dan-john-gilroy) called it "cultural ethnography" within the Star Wars universe. Gilroy said: "Limitations are liberating creatively, by and large."

Limitations ARE liberating — when they're chosen by the writer. When the writer designs the container, the container becomes a creative constraint. When the container is handed down from a spreadsheet, it becomes a cage.

## The Pattern

| Project | Original Format | Final Format | Why It Changed |
|---------|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| *Obi-Wan Kenobi* | Movie trilogy | 6-episode series | Solo bombed, pivot to Disney+ |
| *Mandalorian & Grogu* | TV Season 4 | Feature film | Theatrical strategy shift |
| *The Hobbit* | Two films | Three films | Studio wanted another LOTR |
| *Andor* | 12 episodes (unchanged) | 12 episodes | Gilroy had leverage |

The three failures share a structural cause: the format was chosen by an executive decision disconnected from the story. The one success had a writer with enough power to say "this is the shape the story needs."

## What This Means

The 8-episode streaming season has become the default not because most stories fit 8 episodes, but because it fits a production budget and a binge weekend. The feature film gets greenlit not because the story is cinematic, but because theatrical releases generate different revenue recognition than streaming exclusives.

The writers know this. They take the container they're given and do their best. Sometimes it works anyway — *Obi-Wan Kenobi* has genuine moments, and Ewan McGregor's performance carries the weight of material that should have been tighter. Sometimes it doesn't — *The Hobbit*'s bloat is structural, not fixable by anyone involved.

But the next time you watch a show that feels like it's stalling for time, or a movie that feels like it's sprinting past character moments that needed room to breathe, you're not imagining it. The container came first.

The story came second.
